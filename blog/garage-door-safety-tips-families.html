Garage Door Safety: 8 Essential Tips Every Family Should Know

Your garage door is the largest and heaviest moving object in your home. These safety practices protect the people you love.

Your garage door is the heaviest moving object in your home, weighing between 150 and 400 pounds. It opens and closes roughly 1,500 times per year, and each cycle involves springs under extreme tension, moving cables, and a motor capable of significant force. When everything is functioning properly, the system is remarkably safe. But when components wear out or safety features are neglected, a garage door can become a serious hazard.

According to the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission, garage doors cause an estimated 20,000 injuries per year, including crushed fingers, lacerations, and blunt force trauma. Most of these injuries are preventable with awareness, regular testing, and proper maintenance. Here are eight essential safety practices that every family in Broward and Palm Beach County should follow.

1. Perform a Monthly Auto-Reverse Test

Federal law has required all garage door openers manufactured after January 1, 1993, to include an auto-reverse mechanism. This feature causes the door to immediately reverse direction if it encounters resistance while closing. It is designed to prevent the door from closing on a person, pet, or object in its path. However, the auto-reverse mechanism can weaken or fail over time if not tested regularly.

To test the mechanical auto-reverse, place a two-by-four piece of lumber flat on the ground in the center of the door's path. Press the close button on your opener. When the door contacts the board, it should immediately reverse and return to the fully open position. If it does not reverse, or if it reverses but only after applying significant downward force on the board, the opener's force settings need adjustment or the mechanism needs repair.

This test takes less than 60 seconds and should be performed once per month. Make it part of your household routine, just like checking smoke detector batteries. If the auto-reverse fails the test, stop using the opener and call a professional technician for service before operating the door again.

2. Check Your Photo-Eye Sensors

Photo-eye sensors are the small devices mounted on each side of the garage door opening, approximately six inches above the floor. They emit an invisible infrared beam across the doorway. If anything breaks the beam while the door is closing, the door immediately reverses. This is your second line of defense after the mechanical auto-reverse, and it is specifically designed to detect objects that the door has not yet contacted.

To test your photo-eye sensors, start closing the garage door with the wall button or remote, then wave a broom handle or your leg through the beam path near the sensors. The door should reverse immediately. If it does not, check the following:

  • Alignment: Both sensor units must be pointing directly at each other. Even slight bumps can knock them out of alignment. Most sensors have indicator lights that glow steady green when properly aligned.
  • Cleanliness: South Florida's humidity, pollen, dust, and spider webs can coat the sensor lenses. Wipe them clean with a soft cloth.
  • Obstructions: Ensure no boxes, tools, trash cans, or other items are blocking the beam path.
  • Sun interference: Direct afternoon sunlight can overwhelm the infrared beam. If this is a recurring issue, install sensor shades or reposition the sensors.

Never disable or bypass your photo-eye sensors, even temporarily. If they are malfunctioning, have them repaired promptly.

3. Keep Remotes and Wall Buttons Away from Children

Children are naturally curious, and a garage door remote can look like an interesting toy. Unfortunately, a child pressing the button while another child or pet is near the door can lead to a serious accident. Treat your garage door remote like any other potentially dangerous household item and keep it out of reach of young children.

Specific steps include: mount the wall button at least 5 feet above the floor so small children cannot reach it, keep car-mounted remotes in the locked vehicle rather than on accessible surfaces, use the lockout feature on your opener to disable remotes while the wall button remains active, and teach children early that the garage door is not a toy and should never be run under while in motion. For tech-savvy families, upgrading to a smartphone-controlled opener eliminates physical buttons entirely.

4. Train Every Family Member on the Emergency Release

Every garage door opener has an emergency release mechanism, typically a red cord with a handle that hangs from the trolley on the opener rail. Pulling this cord disconnects the door from the opener, allowing the door to be operated manually. This is essential during power outages, which are common during South Florida's hurricane season, and in emergency situations where the opener is malfunctioning.

Every family member old enough to understand should know:

  • Where the emergency release cord is located and what it looks like
  • How to pull it with a firm, straight-down tug
  • That the door will become very heavy once disconnected, especially if springs are worn, and should be lifted with both hands using proper form
  • How to reconnect the door to the opener after power is restored by pulling the cord toward the motor unit and cycling the opener
  • Never to use the emergency release when the door is open if the springs are broken, as the door could come crashing down under its own weight

Practice using the emergency release at least once per year so that every family member is comfortable with the process. In a hurricane evacuation scenario or a power outage in the middle of the night, you do not want that to be the first time anyone has operated the door manually.

5. Choose Pinch-Resistant Panels

As a garage door opens and closes, the joints between panels flex and create gaps that can pinch fingers. This is one of the most common garage door injuries, particularly among children who may place their hands between panels out of curiosity or while trying to help lift the door manually.

Pinch-resistant panels feature a flush-joint design that eliminates the gap between sections during operation. The panel edges are contoured so that they channel fingers away from the joint rather than trapping them. Most modern garage doors from reputable manufacturers like Clopay, Amarr, and Wayne Dalton include pinch-resistant panel designs as a standard feature.

If your current door has traditional raised-panel joints with visible gaps between sections, this is an important safety consideration when deciding whether to repair or replace your garage door. Upgrading to a door with pinch-resistant panels eliminates one of the most preventable garage door injuries.

6. Install Finger Protection on the Bottom Panel

The bottom edge of the garage door presents a separate pinch and crush hazard. When the door closes, the gap between the bottom panel and the floor narrows rapidly, and fingers placed in this area can be severely injured. This risk is especially acute for toddlers and young children who may sit or play near the base of the door.

Several protective measures can mitigate this risk:

  • Bottom weatherstripping with a safety edge: A thick, flexible rubber seal along the bottom of the door that cushions contact and creates a visible barrier
  • Finger guards or finger shields: Transparent or opaque guards that attach between the bottom two panels, preventing fingers from being inserted into the closing gap
  • Properly adjusted close force: Ensuring your opener's close force is set to the minimum necessary to fully close the door reduces the severity of any contact

Your garage door technician can install these protective accessories during a routine maintenance visit. They are inexpensive additions that provide significant peace of mind for families with small children.

7. Be Aware of Carbon Monoxide Risks

While not directly related to the garage door mechanism itself, carbon monoxide poisoning in attached garages is a serious and often overlooked safety concern. Running a vehicle in a closed garage, even briefly, can generate lethal concentrations of carbon monoxide that seep into the home through shared walls, doorways, and ductwork.

Essential precautions include:

  • Never idle a car in the garage with the door closed. Even with the door open, prolonged idling can allow CO to accumulate in the home through shared walls and ductwork.
  • Install a carbon monoxide detector in the room adjacent to the garage and on every sleeping level. Choose models with digital displays showing real-time CO levels.
  • Never use gas-powered equipment such as generators, grills, or power washers inside the garage, even with the door open.
  • Seal the shared wall between the garage and living space with weatherstripping around the entry door and caulk around pipe or wire penetrations.

Carbon monoxide is colorless and odorless, making it impossible to detect without a proper alarm. In South Florida, where homes are sealed tightly for air conditioning, the risk of CO infiltration from an attached garage is heightened.

8. Schedule Regular Professional Inspections

Even the most diligent homeowner cannot evaluate every component of a garage door system without professional training and tools. Springs under tension, cable conditions, track alignment, roller wear, and opener motor health all require expert assessment. A professional safety inspection should be scheduled at least once per year, and ideally twice per year for doors that receive heavy use.

During a professional inspection, a qualified technician will test the auto-reverse function, verify photo-eye sensor alignment, inspect springs, cables, and drums for wear or corrosion, check track alignment and roller condition, lubricate all moving parts, tighten hardware and brackets, test the emergency release, inspect weatherstripping, evaluate panel condition, and verify the opener's battery backup if equipped.

For families in Broward and Palm Beach County, we recommend scheduling inspections before and after hurricane season to ensure your door is ready for storm conditions and to address any damage from the summer weather cycle.

Take Garage Door Safety Seriously

These eight safety practices are straightforward and can prevent the vast majority of garage door injuries. The monthly reversal test and photo-eye check take under five minutes combined. Teaching children about garage door safety is a one-time conversation that reinforces itself with reminders. And an annual professional inspection is a modest investment that protects both your family and your equipment.
