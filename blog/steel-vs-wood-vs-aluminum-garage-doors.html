Steel vs. Wood vs. Aluminum Garage Doors

An honest, side-by-side comparison to help South Florida homeowners choose the right material.

Buying Guide February 22, 2026 8 min read ProTeam Garage Door

Choosing the right garage door material is one of the most important decisions you will make when upgrading your home. Steel, wood, and aluminum are the three most popular options, and each brings distinct advantages and trade-offs to the table. The right choice depends on your budget, your home's architectural style, how much maintenance you are willing to perform, and — critically for homeowners in Broward and Palm Beach County — how well the material performs in South Florida's demanding coastal climate. This guide provides a comprehensive, head-to-head comparison across every factor that matters so you can make an informed decision.

Durability and Strength

Steel

Steel garage doors are built to take a beating. Available in 24-, 25-, and 26-gauge thicknesses, they resist denting, cracking, and warping under normal use. A quality steel door with a baked-on polyester finish can withstand years of South Florida sun, rain, and humidity without peeling or fading. The primary vulnerability in our region is rust, particularly within a few miles of the coast where salt air accelerates corrosion. A galvanized substrate with a premium paint finish minimizes this risk.

Wood

Wood garage doors are inherently strong and absorb impacts without denting. However, South Florida's high humidity, frequent rain, and intense UV exposure are wood's natural enemies. Without regular sealing and refinishing, wood will swell, warp, crack, and develop mold. The most durable species for our climate include cypress, western red cedar, and mahogany, all with natural resistance to moisture and insects.

Aluminum

Aluminum garage doors will never rust — period. This is their greatest advantage in South Florida's coastal environment and the primary reason they are popular in Fort Lauderdale, Boca Raton, Deerfield Beach, and Jupiter. Aluminum also resists corrosion from salt spray and moisture. The trade-off is that aluminum is softer than steel and more prone to denting. Modern doors use thicker extrusions to mitigate this, but they cannot match steel's impact resistance.

Maintenance Requirements

Steel

Steel doors are the low-maintenance champion. Wash once or twice a year with mild soap, touch up paint chips to prevent rust, and lubricate hardware annually. Modern factory finishes resist fading for 10 to 15 years before repainting becomes necessary.

Wood

Wood is the highest-maintenance option by a wide margin. In South Florida, a wood door needs resealing or restaining every one to two years. Neglecting the finish leads to swelling, warping, rot, and pest infestations. Annual maintenance costs run $200 to $500, and over the door's life, maintenance can exceed the original purchase price.

Aluminum

Aluminum requires the least maintenance of all three. It does not rust, does not need painting, and resists mold and mildew. An occasional wash is all it takes. Anodized and powder-coated finishes maintain their appearance for decades.

Cost Comparison

For a standard 16x7 double-car garage door including professional installation in the Broward and Palm Beach County market, here is what you can expect to pay in 2026:

  • Steel (insulated, hurricane-rated): $2,000 to $4,000 installed
  • Wood (custom, hurricane-reinforced): $5,000 to $8,000+ installed
  • Aluminum with glass panels (hurricane-rated): $4,000 to $7,000+ installed

Steel delivers the best value per dollar for most homeowners. Wood is a premium investment suited to luxury properties where the aesthetic payoff justifies the higher upfront and ongoing costs. Aluminum falls in between, offering a distinctly modern look and zero-rust performance at a price point that is accessible for mid-range to upper-range budgets.

Insulation and Energy Efficiency

Steel

Steel offers the widest insulation range. A triple-layer door with injected polyurethane can achieve R-values of 12 to 18, making it the best choice for energy efficiency. In South Florida, this insulation meaningfully reduces cooling costs when the garage shares a wall with living space.

Wood

Wood is a natural insulator with an inherent R-value of R-4 to R-6. While better than uninsulated alternatives, it falls short of insulated steel. Some manufacturers add polystyrene, but total R-values rarely exceed R-8. Wood's insulating benefit is also offset by gaps that develop as panels expand and contract with moisture.

Aluminum

Aluminum is a poor natural insulator — it actually conducts heat. Uninsulated aluminum offers virtually no thermal resistance. Modern doors with thermal-break technology achieve R-6 to R-8, but full-view glass models offer less unless specified with Low-E glass, which adds to the cost.

Hurricane Resistance

For homeowners in Broward and Palm Beach County, hurricane resistance is not optional — it is mandated by the Florida Building Code. All three materials can be engineered to meet wind-load and impact-resistance requirements, but they do so in different ways and at different price points.

Steel

Steel is the gold standard for hurricane resistance. Its inherent strength and rigidity make it the easiest and most cost-effective material to engineer for high-wind zones. Steel hurricane-rated doors use horizontal and vertical reinforcement struts, heavier-gauge steel, and impact-rated glazing. The hurricane premium on a steel door is typically the lowest of the three materials, adding $300 to $600 to the base price.

Wood

Wood can meet hurricane codes, but it requires significantly more reinforcement than steel. Internal steel frames, thicker panels, and impact-rated window inserts add weight and cost. Wood hurricane-rated doors are among the heaviest on the market, requiring more powerful openers and heavier-duty spring systems. The hurricane premium on a wood door can add $800 to $1,500 to the price.

Aluminum

Aluminum hurricane-rated doors use thicker extrusions, reinforced corners, and impact-rated laminated glass. They are lighter than both steel and wood hurricane doors, which means less stress on the opener and hardware. However, the cost of impact-rated glass panels drives the price higher than standard steel options. The hurricane premium on an aluminum-and-glass door typically adds $500 to $1,000.

Curb Appeal and Style Options

Steel

Modern steel doors are available in an enormous variety of styles: raised panel, recessed panel, carriage house, flush, and more. They can be painted any color and finished with realistic wood-grain textures, decorative hardware, window inserts, and arched top sections. Steel is the most versatile material when it comes to matching different architectural styles, from traditional Mediterranean to contemporary minimalist.

Wood

Nothing matches the warmth and character of real wood. The natural grain patterns, the depth of a hand-applied stain, and the artisanal feel of a custom-crafted wood door create an emotional connection that no synthetic material can fully replicate. Wood doors are the top choice for high-end homes, historic properties, and homeowners who value craftsmanship above all else. Popular species for South Florida include cypress, mahogany, cedar, and reclaimed teak.

Aluminum

Aluminum doors own the modern and contemporary aesthetic. The signature full-view design — slim aluminum frames with large glass panels — has become synonymous with high-end modern architecture. Aluminum frames are available in anodized finishes (clear, dark bronze, black) and powder-coated colors. While aluminum excels in modern settings, it can feel out of place on traditional, colonial, or Mediterranean-style homes.

Climate Suitability for South Florida

South Florida's climate presents a unique combination of challenges: intense UV radiation, high humidity, salt air, frequent rain, and hurricane-force winds. Here is how each material holds up:

  • Steel performs very well overall but needs rust-prevention attention within five miles of the coast. Choose a galvanized substrate with a premium paint finish for coastal locations.
  • Wood struggles the most in our climate. The constant humidity and rain cycle causes swelling and contraction that stresses joints and finishes. Wood is best suited to covered or partially sheltered garage openings where direct rain exposure is limited.
  • Aluminum is arguably the best-suited material for coastal South Florida. It shrugs off salt spray, humidity, and rain without any degradation. It is the go-to choice for beachfront and waterfront properties.

Lifespan: How Long Will Each Material Last?

Assuming proper maintenance and normal use:

  • Steel: 20 to 30 years. The door itself can last even longer; it is usually the hardware, springs, and opener that need replacement first.
  • Wood: 15 to 25 years with diligent maintenance. Without regular sealing and refinishing, the lifespan can shrink to as little as 10 years in South Florida's climate.
  • Aluminum: 25 to 35 years. Aluminum's resistance to rust and corrosion gives it the longest potential lifespan of the three materials, particularly in coastal environments.

Our Recommendation for South Florida Homeowners

For the majority of homeowners in Broward and Palm Beach County, an insulated steel garage door delivers the best combination of durability, energy efficiency, hurricane resistance, style options, and value. It is the most popular choice in our market for good reason.

If you live on or near the water and want a modern aesthetic, an aluminum and glass door is worth the investment for its unmatched corrosion resistance and contemporary style.

If you own a luxury or historic home and are willing to invest in ongoing maintenance, a custom wood door will deliver a level of warmth and character that no other material can match.
